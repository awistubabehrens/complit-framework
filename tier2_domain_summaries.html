1
Foundations of Artificial Intelligence Literacy
Knowledge and skills to understand what AI is, how it works, recognize its presence and influence, evaluate AI outputs critically, and distinguish between human and AI capabilities.
Why this domain matters
AI systems now shape decisions in hiring, healthcare, education, and civic life. Students who cannot recognize AI, evaluate its outputs, or understand its limitations are not positioned to participate fully as citizens or workers. This domain builds the foundational literacy every student needs — regardless of whether they pursue computing as a career.

2
Computational Thinking
Knowledge and skills to collaboratively approach complex problems systematically — breaking them down into manageable parts, identifying recurring patterns, extracting essential information, developing step-by-step solutions, and analyzing and representing data to drive informed decision-making.
Why this domain matters
Computational thinking is the problem-solving engine underneath all of CS and AI. Research shows it transfers across disciplines — students with strong CT skills approach challenges in science, math, and humanities more effectively. It is the rigorous technical foundation that makes the other domains meaningful.

2.3Abstraction & Generalization ✦Extract essential information, ignore irrelevant details, create models that capture key characteristics, and develop generalizable solutions.

2.7Creativity & Design Thinking ✦Apply creative problem-solving approaches, design thinking processes, and innovation methodologies to generate novel computing solutions.
3
Computer Science
Knowledge and skills to harness computing power for complex problem-solving, innovation, and digital landscape shaping — enabling the creation and critical analysis of solutions in a world driven by data, algorithms, and artificial intelligence systems.
Why this domain matters
Computer Science is the technical core — writing and debugging code, understanding systems, working with data structures, and building real products. It is the domain most associated with the CS field's career pipeline, and also the one where access inequities are sharpest. Rigorous, high-quality CS education for every student is a matter of equity.

4
Computing
Knowledge and skills to navigate and utilize digital environments, select and use appropriate digital tools including AI systems, efficiently manage information, solve common technical issues, and leverage computer systems to enhance productivity and effectiveness.
Why this domain matters
Computing is the everyday layer — the skills students use to function effectively in digital environments and work productively with AI tools. It bridges the technical (CS) with the practical (Digital Literacy), ensuring students are not just consumers of technology but intentional, capable operators of it.

5
Digital Literacy
Knowledge and skills to practice responsible digital citizenship, understand ethical implications of technology and AI, critically evaluate information and media sources for credibility and bias, analyze technology's impact on society, and respect intellectual property and digital rights.
Why this domain matters
Digital literacy is the civic and ethical dimension of computing education. As AI amplifies both the reach and the risks of digital systems, students need robust skills to evaluate what they encounter, protect themselves and others, and engage responsibly. This domain is essential for whole-child development and for preparing students to be active, discerning participants in a technology-shaped society.

6
Career Learning
Knowledge and skills to explore computing and AI careers across diverse fields, understand how automation and AI transform work including job displacement and creation, and develop professional collaboration and teamwork skills for working effectively in diverse technology teams.
Why this domain matters
Career connections increase student motivation, broaden participation from underrepresented groups, and prepare students for workforce realities. Research shows strong career pathways require iterative skill-building with clear linkages between school and work — and that students, especially those from communities historically excluded from tech, benefit most from seeing diverse CS careers made visible and accessible.

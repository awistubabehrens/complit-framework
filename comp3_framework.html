Whole-child focus
Instead of: Technical and applied CS skills with little attention to holistic well-being
Students develop CS and AI knowledge through kinesthetic activities, build emotional and social skills through digital citizenship and self-reflection, and experience safety, comfort, and privacy in online and in-person environments.
Connection & community
Instead of: Individual practice and competition
Students collaborate frequently with peers and community on CS challenges — coding projects, design challenges, community-based tech solutions. Every learner's identity is a strength. Perspectives are accepted, respected, and celebrated, especially across different ability levels.
High expectations & rigor
Instead of: Limited access for a select few, rote tasks, memorization
Every student — regardless of background or prior exposure — accesses high-quality CS and AI learning at grade level. Tasks demand rigorous thinking: analyzing data, recognizing patterns, synthesizing knowledge, and applying it in real-world contexts while maintaining critical judgment of AI outputs.
Relevance
Instead of: Standardized, hypothetical scenarios that "prepare" students for later
Students engage in meaningful tasks that build on their interests, use cutting-edge technology including AI, and connect to real-world problems now. Materials reflect their backgrounds, cultures, and communities. They experience how human creativity and technology shape current careers and society.
Customization
Instead of: Standardized instruction regardless of need
Learning structures are flexible — how, when, where, and from whom students engage is based on what supports them best. Content is tailored to individual needs, interests, and goals. AI and computing tools provide scaffolds that get students unstuck faster. Multiple pathways exist to demonstrate mastery.
Agency
Instead of: Passive absorption of content
Students take ownership of their CS and AI learning — choosing how they learn, making personal connections, exploring technology's impact on society, and building socially responsible solutions. They document and share outputs, contribute to classroom and community dialogue, and take action to positively impact their communities.

1
Foundations of Artificial Intelligence Literacy
Knowledge and skills to understand what AI is, how it works, recognize its presence and influence, evaluate AI outputs critically, and distinguish between human and AI capabilities.
Why this domain matters
AI systems now shape decisions in hiring, healthcare, education, and civic life. Students who cannot recognize AI, evaluate its outputs, or understand its limitations are not positioned to participate fully as citizens or workers. This domain builds the foundational literacy every student needs — regardless of whether they pursue computing as a career.

Computational + AI Literacy · Design Framework

The student experience

Effective COMP3+AI programs require three essential parts working together. Each serves a distinct function, and together they ensure every student has access to rigorous, supportive, and enriching computing education.

The distinction between core, intervention, and enrichment is not whether students engage in these types of learning — but rather the level of support, intensity, time, and complexity each requires. All three parts share the same four-phase learning cycle: Orientation to Learning → Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills → Practice & Demonstration → Feedback & Reflection.
Core Instruction
Enrichment
Intervention
What it is
Core instruction establishes the foundation for learning in COMP3 and AI. It is the primary, grade-level learning experience that all students participate in and is designed to meet the needs of the vast majority of learners.
Why it matters
When core instruction is strong and delivered at grade level, more students gain access to rigorous computing learning without being tracked out of opportunity. Because computing learning is cumulative, core instruction is essential for establishing the foundational knowledge students need to succeed over time. Seemingly small gaps — misunderstandings about variables, conditionals, functions, or data structures — can quickly block future learning. Core instruction also builds the shared language and practices that interventions and enrichment rely on, such as debugging routines, code readability expectations, and responsible AI decision-making.
The four-phase learning cycle in core instruction
① Orientation to Learning
  • Clarifies learning goals, success criteria, and relevance — why a programming concept or AI system matters in the real world.
  • Establishes a belief that all students can learn COMP3 and AI, countering stereotypes about who belongs in computing.
  • Connects new learning to prior knowledge and future applications.
  • All students are expected to master the same standards, though the exact sequence and timeline may differ from student to student.
② Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills
  • Introduces new concepts and skills through explicit modeling, worked examples, and developmentally appropriate inquiry.
  • Uses various approaches — visuals, code walkthroughs, discussion, simulations — to reduce cognitive load.
  • Balances direct instruction with guided exploration as students build initial mental models.
③ Practice & Demonstration
  • Provides frequent, scaffolded opportunities to apply learning — debugging routines, short builds, data explorations.
  • Includes both deliberate practice for fluency and authentic tasks for application.
  • Encourages collaboration (e.g., pair programming) when it supports focus and learning goals.
  • Students' outputs or how they show mastery may look different from one another.
④ Feedback & Reflection
  • Provides timely, specific feedback aligned with clear criteria — code quality, reasoning, process, and responsible use.
  • Includes opportunities to revise work and reflect on strategies used.
  • Builds student self-efficacy by making progress visible.
Progression across grade bands
Elementary (K–5)
Foundational computational thinking, block-based programming, unplugged activities, and early AI concepts — classification, patterns, fairness. Strong emphasis on orientation and modeling. Offered as a core class or integrated into STEM.
Middle School (6–8)
Increased focus on programming constructs, data representation, and structured practice. AI learning includes simple model-building and evaluation with reflection on limitations. Offered as a core class or integrated into STEM.
High School (9–12)
More complex systems, data pipelines, and responsible AI use. Core instruction supports sustained projects while preserving explicit feedback and reflection. Offered as a core class, integrated into STEM, or through defined pathways.
What it is
Enrichment raises the ceiling — extending learning beyond the core experience by increasing depth, complexity, creativity, and authenticity. It provides opportunities for students to explore COMP3+AI in more open-ended ways while building identity, motivation, and a sense of agency as capable problem-solvers and creators.
Why it matters
Enrichment helps students deepen understanding and transfer core skills into new contexts, strengthening long-term learning and flexible problem-solving. In COMP3 and AI, enrichment is a powerful driver of identity development: students are more likely to persist in computing pathways when they experience autonomy, meaningful purpose, and opportunities to create work that matters to them or their communities. Enrichment creates space for curiosity, innovation, and challenge — helping students move beyond "getting the code to work" toward designing, refining, and communicating solutions. It also supports responsible AI literacy by giving students structured opportunities to explore how AI systems behave, where they fall short, and what ethical considerations come with using them.
The four-phase learning cycle in enrichment
① Orientation to Learning
  • Centers student choice, purpose, and relevance — "What problem do you want to solve with computing or AI?"
  • Connects learning to real-world meaning, community issues, or student interests.
  • Builds motivation and ownership by allowing students to define success and set goals.
  • Students know how their enrichment learning connects to the standards in core instruction.
② Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills
  • Introduces advanced or extended content connected to core learning — APIs, new libraries, model evaluation ideas, new data sources.
  • Provides optional scaffolds so students can stretch without getting stuck.
  • Encourages exploration through structured inquiry rather than step-by-step replication.
③ Practice & Demonstration
  • Emphasizes authentic application through longer builds — projects, prototypes, investigations, or community-based challenges.
  • Expands what "demonstration" can look like: code, design artifacts, testing plans, user research, ethical analyses.
  • Encourages iteration, experimentation, and problem-solving under real constraints.
  • How students demonstrate mastery may look different from one another.
④ Feedback & Reflection
  • Incorporates feedback from multiple perspectives — peers, teachers, mentors, end users, community members.
  • Emphasizes reflection on both product and process: what changed, why decisions were made, tradeoffs, and impact.
  • Builds metacognition, communication skills, and a deeper computing identity — "I am someone who can build, test, and improve systems."
Progression across grade bands
Elementary (K–5)
Enrichment often looks like creative computing — interactive stories, games, animations, or simple AI-inspired sorting and classifying activities tied to student interests and play.
Middle School (6–8)
Students engage in mini-projects with data and AI tools — experimenting with classification, critiquing limitations, refining designs — often in collaborative teams.
High School (9–12)
Enrichment can expand into capstones, advanced electives, community-based projects, research experiences, internships, competitions, or deeper AI systems work with strong ethical and safety guardrails.
What it is
Intervention acts as a safety net — providing targeted, additional support for students who are not yet making sufficient progress in core COMP3+AI learning. It is designed to address specific learning needs without replacing access to grade-level instruction.
Why it matters
Computing learning is cumulative, and even small misunderstandings — confusion about variables, Boolean logic, conditionals, loops, functions, or data handling — can quickly become barriers to future learning. Within a Multi-Tiered System of Supports (MTSS) framework, intervention serves as a responsive layer of support that helps students strengthen foundational skills while staying connected to Tier 1 grade-level goals. High-quality intervention also protects student confidence and persistence: programming often includes frequent "failure states" (bugs, errors, unexpected outputs), and targeted support paired with rapid feedback can rebuild self-efficacy and reduce disengagement.
The four-phase learning cycle in intervention
① Orientation to Learning
  • Clarifies the specific skill focus and why it matters — "Today we're working on loops because they help us automate patterns in our programs."
  • Reinforces that growth is expected and achievable, supporting student confidence and persistence.
  • Makes success criteria concrete and visible — "I can trace this code and explain what happens step by step."
  • Students target specific standards based on their unique needs.
② Exposure to New Knowledge & Skills
  • Re-introduces key ideas using different representations or more explicit teaching — diagrams, tracing tables, worked examples.
  • Slows pacing and reduces cognitive load by chunking content into smaller steps.
  • Provides guided modeling of thinking processes students may not yet do independently — debugging moves, predicting output, reading error messages.
③ Practice & Demonstration
  • Focuses practice on a small set of high-leverage tasks — tracing conditionals, fixing one bug type, writing one function pattern.
  • Provides repetition and structured support to build fluency and reduce overwhelm.
  • Uses short, frequent practice cycles that allow students to make progress quickly.
  • How students demonstrate mastery may look different from one another.
④ Feedback & Reflection
  • Provides rapid, actionable feedback with immediate opportunities to revise and succeed.
  • Helps students name what strategy worked — "I used print statements to locate the bug."
  • Makes progress visible so students can see growth and regain momentum.
Progression across grade bands
Elementary (K–5)
Supports rely on concrete and multimodal approaches — unplugged routines, block sequencing cards, guided tool use — to strengthen early logic and sequencing skills.
Middle School (6–8)
Common intervention focuses include debugging routines, conditional reasoning, variable tracking, and understanding code structure through tracing and explanation.
High School (9–12)
Targeted clinics may focus on functions, scope, data structures, testing and debugging workflows, and for AI: dataset quality, evaluation basics, and interpretation of model behavior.
Key design principles for intervention
Intervention is additive, not replacement.
Intervention should protect access to grade-level COMP3+AI instruction rather than replace it. Students in intervention still participate in core instruction.
Curriculum alignment is essential.
Intervention tasks should mirror the language, representations, and expectations of core instruction so students aren't learning in a disconnected system.
Staffing matters.
Teachers play a key role in diagnosing needs through student work. Interventionists, specialists, or trained support staff can deliver high-impact small-group sessions using aligned protocols and materials.

Curriculum, Instruction & Assessment
What students learn, how they learn it, and how learning is measured.
Leaps most connected
High expectations & rigorRelevanceAgencyCustomization
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Curriculum addresses all COMP3 learning outcomes with clear progression.
Students build knowledge and skills across grade levels through vetted curricula that develop all six domains — not isolated units, but a coherent K–12 sequence.
Instruction uses varied approaches and activities.
Students learn through explicit instruction, hands-on coding and unplugged activities, collaborative projects, inquiry-based problem-solving, industry experiences, peer discussions, and real-world applications that integrate AI tools and connect to their lives.
Assessment is ongoing and authentic.
Teachers track progress through outcome-aligned exit tickets, project evaluations, peer feedback, self-reflection, class discussions, and diagnostics. Assessments are curriculum-based rather than waiting for standardized tests.
AI literacy is woven throughout instruction.
Students learn how AI works, its applications and limitations, ethical considerations, and how to use AI tools responsibly — embedded throughout the learning sequence, not added on.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Available curricula rarely cover all learning outcomes. Most require stitching multiple resources together, which risks gaps and incoherence.
Design response
Map available curricula against the six outcome domains explicitly. Identify gaps and plan targeted supplementation or custom units for uncovered areas.
Challenge
Traditional CS instruction tends to be isolating rather than collaborative, with students working independently on screens.
Design response
Build in pair programming, group projects, and structured discussions as core instructional strategies. Collaboration is a learning outcome, not a reward.

School & Classroom Culture, Routines & Norms
The shared beliefs, expectations, and patterns of interaction that shape how students experience learning every day.
Leaps most connected
Whole-child focusConnection & communityAgency
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Every student belongs in computing.
Inclusive language, diverse role models, and celebrating varied approaches signal to every student — especially those historically excluded from CS — that they are capable computer scientists and AI users.
COMP3 connects to real-world topics students care about.
Computational thinking is grounded in issues students find meaningful — climate change, social justice, community needs, and AI ethics. CS topics that connect the real world to the school community are visualized throughout the school.
Routines build collaboration and digital wellness.
Classroom norms emphasize peer support and productive struggle. Schools promote healthy technology habits, balanced screen time, and mindful use of digital tools including AI.
AI and emerging technologies are ongoing conversations.
Students, families, and staff engage regularly in discussions about AI's impact, ethical computing, and responsible technology use — woven into school culture, not limited to one-time lessons.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
CS culture often defaults to a narrow "programmer" identity that excludes students who don't self-identify with tech — particularly girls, students of color, and students from under-resourced communities.
Design response
Actively surface diverse computing role models, celebrate multiple ways of solving problems, and connect CS to domains students already care about. Representation in materials and classroom visuals matters.
Challenge
Productive failure — essential in CS — can undermine student confidence if classrooms aren't built to normalize struggle and iteration.
Design response
Build explicit norms around debugging, revision, and learning from errors. Frame "my code doesn't work" as a normal, expected part of the process — not a sign of incapacity.

Schedules & Use of Time
How instructional time is allocated, structured, and protected for CS and AI learning.
Leaps most connected
High expectations & rigorRelevanceCustomization
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Adequate time is allocated for COMP3 instruction.
Students engage in 2–5 hours per week of COMP3 instruction. The master schedule protects this time with the same priority as core subjects.
COMP3 is integrated across subjects and schedules.
Computational thinking and AI literacy are woven into math, science, ELA, and social studies — not siloed to a single period. Cross-curricular integration reinforces learning and increases total exposure.
Flexible instructional structures allow for differentiation.
Station rotations, weekly digital citizenship lessons, quarterly COMP3 projects, and CS explorations give teachers tools to reach different learners across core instruction, enrichment, and intervention.
Professional learning time is built into the schedule.
Staff schedules include dedicated time for COMP3 training, collaborative planning, and ongoing learning about emerging technologies including AI.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
CS lacks core subject status in most states, making it easy to cut when schedules are tight or competing priorities emerge.
Design response
Anchor CS time to existing schedule structures. Make it non-negotiable at the master schedule level, visible in the school's public communication about what it offers.

Adult Roles, Hiring & Learning
What adults do, what they need to know and believe, how they are hired, and how they are supported to grow.
Leaps most connected
High expectations & rigorConnection & communityWhole-child focus
Goals for this element
  • Educators believe deeply in every student's potential to excel in computing and feel shared responsibility to broaden participation — regardless of background, prior achievement, or whether students see themselves as "tech people."
  • All educators responsible for COMP3 instruction possess strong content knowledge and pedagogical expertise, including the ability to anticipate students' thinking and address misconceptions.
  • Collaboration among educators is routine and purposeful — grounded in shared materials and student work, enabling consistent practice across classrooms.
  • Professional learning is ongoing, curriculum-embedded, and directly connected to upcoming instruction — sustained long enough to meaningfully build CS content knowledge and self-efficacy.
  • Educators and leaders understand that COMP3 education is a rapidly evolving field, and professional learning systems are designed to keep pace with changes in content, pedagogy, tools, and AI.
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
COMP3 leadership roles are clearly established.
A specialized COMP3 teacher with CS expertise, an IT specialist for tech management, and a STEM+COMP3 administrator oversee program development. Principals, APs, and instructional coaches actively support COMP3 implementation.
Professional development is continuous, curriculum-aligned, and AI-inclusive.
Teachers receive training that builds capacity to understand AI tools, teach AI concepts appropriately, and integrate AI responsibly into COMP3 instruction. PD includes coaching cycles, lesson internalization protocols, and PLCs.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Only 48% of teachers currently feel equipped to teach AI, and 88% say they need more professional learning resources. AI advances faster than most PD systems can keep up.
Design response
Build a sustained, multi-year PD system rather than one-time workshops. Partner with curriculum providers for embedded support. Connect educators to professional networks like CSTA.
Challenge
CS educators are often isolated — the only person in their building teaching the subject — which limits collaboration, coaching, and shared improvement.
Design response
Create cross-school or cross-district PLCs for CS educators. Use virtual collaboration tools to connect isolated teammates. Designate a CS instructional lead even in small settings.

Technology & Tech Infrastructure
Devices, connectivity, software, and the systems that keep them working equitably and reliably.
Leaps most connected
High expectations & rigorCustomizationRelevance
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Equitable access to devices and connectivity is guaranteed.
A 1:1 device-to-student ratio with reliable WiFi at school and home. Device refresh schedules prevent equity gaps from widening over time as hardware ages.
Technology procurement is aligned to curriculum.
All hardware and software purchases directly support curriculum requirements and are sustainable beyond individual teachers. AI platforms and software are evaluated for long-term viability — not just grant-period coverage.
Systems manage technology reliably.
An LMS organizes coursework, clear procedures govern device distribution and security, and digital citizenship policies are in place. Tech support with clear troubleshooting protocols serves students, families, and staff.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
AI educational tools proliferate faster than schools can evaluate them. Without quality control frameworks, schools risk adopting tools that are pedagogically weak, inequitable, or unsustainable.
Design response
Use the Curriculum Evaluator Tool and AI Curriculum Airtable to assess tools against criteria including alignment to COMP3 outcomes, equity features, and evidence of effectiveness.

Space & Facilities
How physical learning environments are designed, arranged, and equipped to support the full range of COMP3 learning modes.
Leaps most connected
Connection & communityHigh expectations & rigorCustomization
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Spaces support the full range of COMP3 learning modes.
Effective computing spaces accommodate pair programming, group projects, design sprints, debugging sessions, hardware building, and whole-class discussion. A traditional fixed-row computer lab serves none of these well.
Physical computing materials are treated as core infrastructure.
Robotics kits, microcontrollers, sensors, and maker tools are budgeted on the same cycle as textbooks, with assigned maintenance responsibility and documented checkout systems.
Flexible furniture enables multiple configurations.
Modular furniture reconfigurable in under two minutes, power access across the room, large shared displays visible from all positions, adequate material storage, and writable surfaces for collaborative design.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Computing educators often inherit spaces not designed for their pedagogy. Making the case for reconfiguration requires explaining why a "computer lab" is insufficient.
Design response
Audit existing spaces against the full range of COMP3 learning modes. Use the audit to generate specific renovation requests. Spatial disparities are a learning equity issue.

Family & Community Partnerships
How schools engage caregivers and communities as partners in students' computing and AI learning.
Leaps most connected
Connection & communityRelevanceAgency
Goals for this element
  • Families are informed early, often, and specifically about what AI is being used in class, why, and what student data it touches — recognizing that as of 2025, 96% of families with elementary-aged children reported receiving no AI-related communication from their school.
  • Communication about CS and AI instruction is bidirectional and ongoing — allowing families to ask questions and share concerns as tools evolve.
  • Families are meaningfully engaged as partners in their children's computing and AI learning — with opportunities to understand what students are doing, why it matters, and how to support learning at home.
  • Engagement practices are designed to reach all families, including those facing language barriers, schedule constraints, limited digital access, or historical marginalization from school systems.
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Families are partners, not recipients of information.
COMP3 nights, workshops on supporting computational thinking at home, and opportunities to participate in showcases position caregivers as active participants. Their expertise about their children and communities informs the design.
Real-world learning connects students to community and careers.
Guest speakers, workplace tours, student mentorships, and community-based projects connect students to COMP3 applications and career pathways — making learning relevant and broadening participation.
AI communication is proactive and transparent.
Schools provide regular updates when new tools are introduced, explain what data is collected and how it is protected, and offer ready-made communication templates so messaging quality doesn't depend on individual teacher initiative.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Many caregivers have limited technical backgrounds and may experience AI anxiety — which can translate into skepticism about classroom practices if schools don't address it directly.
Design response
Offer adult-only sessions where caregivers experience CS and AI activities themselves. Acknowledge uncertainty openly — families who feel included in the learning process trust schools more than those who feel information is being managed.

Budget & Operations
How resources are allocated, sustained, and diversified to support a durable COMP3+AI program.
Leaps most connected
High expectations & rigorRelevance
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
COMP3 essentials are funded sustainably.
Budgets cover technology, staff, professional development, curriculum, and infrastructure — annually, not just in year one. Initial and ongoing costs are planned explicitly rather than assumed to decrease.
AI tool funding extends beyond grant periods.
New AI platforms and software are evaluated for sustainability. Schools avoid building dependence on short-term funding that disappears in 2–3 years, displacing both tools and the teacher capacity built around them.
Staffing resources are allocated strategically.
Dedicated COMP3 educators, specialists across grade levels, planning time for integration, instructional coaches, and coordinators for secondary pathways are funded as core positions — not add-ons.
Funding sources are diverse.
Local partnerships, state and federal grants (including NSF and USDOE investments in AI education), and community fundraising supplement core allocations — reducing vulnerability to any single funding source.

Continuous Learning & Improvement
The systems and routines used to define success, measure progress, and reflect together to improve quality and impact.
Leaps most connected
High expectations & rigorConnection & community
Goals for this element
  • Improvement efforts are sustained, focused, and cumulative — schools select a small number of high-leverage instructional priorities and pursue them over multiple years.
  • Assessment practices serve instructional purposes — providing timely, actionable feedback to both students and educators about conceptual understanding, computational thinking, and coding proficiency.
  • Equity is an explicit and ongoing object of continuous improvement — using participation and outcomes data to identify and address structural barriers.
  • CS and AI improvement efforts connect to broader professional networks and research-practice partnerships that supply curriculum-aligned assessments and cross-school learning.
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Data systems are aligned to COMP3 learning outcomes.
LMS and data review processes track COMP3 learning outcomes, culture, perception, and student engagement — not just usage metrics.
Evaluation and adaptation happen regularly.
COMP3 classroom observations, data review meetings, and surveys monitor implementation fidelity, workload, and educator mindsets. Findings drive adjustments, not just documentation.
AI tool effectiveness is continuously evaluated.
How AI platforms and tools are used in instruction, their impact on student learning and educator practice, and stakeholder feedback on AI integration all inform ongoing decisions.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Assessment instruments for CS and AI literacy are still in development. Districts often evaluate AI education through usage metrics rather than learning outcomes.
Design response
Develop curriculum-based assessments rather than waiting for standardized tests. Use student work, debugging logs, and project portfolios as evidence of learning. Connect to research-practice partnerships developing better instruments.
Transcend partners with communities to create and spread extraordinary learning for all.

Understanding AI Systems ✦
Comprehend AI capabilities, limitations, and how AI systems operate — including machine learning, training data, and pattern recognition.

Standard
Explain that computers and AI systems use data to respond to inputs and perform tasks.
Explain how AI learns from data examples and identify AI in everyday applications.
Describe how machine learning systems are trained, evaluate model limitations, and investigate an AI system's behavior.
Train, test, and evaluate AI models; analyze how data choices affect outcomes, fairness, and accuracy.
Description
Students recognize basic differences between regular programs and AI, understanding that AI responds differently based on what it has learned.
Students define AI, explain how it learns from data, and recognize it in recommendation systems, voice assistants, and image recognition tools.
Students investigate an AI system's capabilities and limitations, describe machine learning concepts, and examine how training data shapes model behavior.
Students train a sentiment analysis or classification model, test with new data, evaluate bias and accuracy, and propose enhancements based on results.
Show classroom examples ↓
Example
Students observe a smart speaker and explain: "The AI listens, finds information, and tells us the answer." They discuss other AI they see — like videos being recommended.
Students define AI as "technology that finds patterns in lots of information to predict things." They examine a recommendation system and explain how it collects data and predicts preferences.
Students investigate an image recognition AI. They create a model card: what it was trained to identify, what training data was used, its limitations, and potential biases.
Students train a sentiment analysis AI on social media data. They test with new inputs, evaluate for bias across groups, and propose improvements.

Standard
Ask questions about AI-generated content and identify whether information from AI is correct or makes sense.
Compare strengths and weaknesses of AI tools, evaluate whether AI outputs are factual, and determine when AI is appropriate to use.
Critically evaluate AI outputs for bias, accuracy, limitations, and potential harms, and experiment with different inputs to understand how they affect outputs.

Description

Show classroom examples ↓
Example
After using an AI drawing tool, students discuss: "Did the AI draw what I asked for?" They practice asking "Is this right?" about AI-generated answers.
Students use an AI chatbot for research, verify facts with multiple sources, and identify that AI is helpful for brainstorming but may not be accurate for historical dates.
Students test an AI text generator with different prompts on the same topic, document output changes, spot biased language, and create class guidelines for when to trust AI responses.
Students evaluate a college admissions AI system — analyzing predictions across demographic groups, identifying biases, measuring fairness metrics, and recommending improvements.

Human vs. AI Capabilities ✦
Distinguish between activities humans do well and those AI systems do well, including differences in learning approaches and determining appropriate human involvement.
Show levels ↓
Level 1

Level 2

Level 3

Standard
Describe activities that humans do well and activities that computers do well.
Differentiate between tasks that humans perform well and tasks that AI performs well, and explain why certain tasks require human judgment.
Distinguish between how humans learn and how AI systems learn, and evaluate when human expertise is essential versus when AI augmentation is helpful.
Analyze the complementary strengths of humans and AI, evaluate human agency in AI-driven systems, and make informed decisions about appropriate human involvement in AI workflows.
Description
Students recognize basic differences: humans are good at creative thinking and caring; computers are fast at calculations and following rules.
Students understand that humans excel at creative problem-solving, empathy, and complex decision-making while AI excels at pattern recognition and data processing.
Students compare human learning (concepts, transfer, few examples) with AI learning (pattern recognition from large datasets) and determine appropriate human-AI collaboration.
Students examine AI's limitations in ethical reasoning and creativity. They design systems that preserve meaningful human control in critical decisions.
Show classroom examples ↓
Example
Students sort activities into "humans do better" (comforting friends, creative tasks) and "computers do better" (fast calculations, memory, repetition).
Students create a chart comparing AI strengths (sorting images) with human strengths (understanding emotions and fairness), then discuss why teachers — not AI — should handle classroom conflicts.
Students compare how they learn to identify birds (observation, field trips) with how AI learns (labeled images), then design an app combining AI recognition with human expert verification.
Students analyze medical diagnostic AI, comparing AI strengths (pattern detection) with human expertise (patient context), then propose a workflow with appropriate human oversight safeguards.

Problem Decomposition
Break complex problems into manageable components and create modular solutions that can be developed and tested independently.
Show levels ↓
Level 1
K–2
Level 2
3–5
Level 3
6–8
Level 4
9–12
Standard
Break simple problems into smaller, manageable steps that can be completed in order.
Decompose multi-step problems into smaller subproblems that can be solved independently and then combined.
Systematically decompose complex problems, identify dependencies between subproblems, and create modular solutions that can be developed and tested independently.
Apply advanced decomposition strategies to ill-defined problems, design modular architectures, and evaluate trade-offs in problem decomposition approaches.
Description
Students learn to think about problems as a series of steps, identifying what needs to happen first, next, and last to accomplish a goal.
Students break complex tasks into parts, solve each part separately, and understand how the parts work together to solve the whole problem.
Students analyze problem complexity, identify which parts depend on others, create modular designs where components can be developed separately, and integrate solutions.
Students tackle ambiguous problems, apply decomposition strategies, design modular systems, and evaluate approaches for maintainability, efficiency, and collaboration.
Show classroom examples ↓
Example
Students plan how to make a peanut butter sandwich by listing steps in order. They practice ordering steps for daily routines like getting ready for school.
Students plan a class fundraiser by breaking it into parts: deciding what to sell, calculating costs, creating advertising, organizing volunteers. Each team handles one part, then combines their work.
Students design a school website by decomposing it into modules (navigation, home page, clubs, calendar, contact form). They identify dependencies and assign teams to each module.
Students decompose school food waste into systems (measurement, behavior, operations, donations), design modular solutions with shared data, and evaluate strategies for iteration and collaboration.

Standard
Follow rules for safe and kind online behavior, including getting permission before sharing information and being respectful to others.
Practice responsible digital citizenship: protect personal information, understand digital footprints, demonstrate online etiquette, and respond appropriately to cyberbullying.
Demonstrate digital citizenship: manage digital identity, protect privacy and security, engage ethically online, address cyberbullying, and understand legal implications.
Exhibit exemplary digital citizenship: strategically manage identity, implement advanced security, lead ethically online, and navigate complex legal and ethical issues.
Description
Students learn basic online safety and etiquette, understand the importance of asking permission, and practice being kind in digital interactions.

Students actively manage online presence, implement strong security practices, participate constructively in digital communities, and understand consequences of digital actions.
Students strategically curate digital presence for future opportunities, mentor others, navigate ethical dilemmas, and understand nuanced legal issues.
Show classroom examples ↓
Example
Students learn rules like "ask before sharing photos" and "never share passwords." They practice telling a trusted adult when encountering uncomfortable content.
Students learn their digital footprint is permanent. They create strong passwords, identify safe vs. private information, and practice appropriate etiquette for email and video calls.
Students conduct digital footprint audits, strengthen security with two-factor authentication, and create response guides for online situations.
Students create strategic digital presence plans for college and career, implement comprehensive security, and lead digital citizenship initiatives in their school.

School Elements

These elements are the major levers for design. Every decision a school makes about curriculum, staffing, time, space, technology, culture, and community shapes the student experience — and therefore the outcomes students develop.

This guide is standalone but references the six leaps and six learning outcome domains throughout. For full detail on leaps, see Tier 1. For outcomes, see Tier 2 and Tier 3.

Curriculum, Instruction & Assessment
What students learn, how they learn it, and how learning is measured.
Leaps most connected to this element
High expectations & rigor Relevance Agency Customization
Outcomes this element most directly develops
All 6 domains
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Curriculum addresses all COMP3 learning outcomes with clear progression.
Students build knowledge and skills across grade levels through vetted curricula that develop computational thinking, computer science, digital literacy, AI literacy, computing skills, and career learning — not isolated units, but a coherent K–12 sequence.
Instruction uses varied approaches and activities.
Students learn through explicit instruction, hands-on coding and unplugged activities, collaborative projects, inquiry-based problem-solving, industry experiences, peer discussions, and real-world applications that integrate AI tools and connect to their lives.
Assessment is ongoing and authentic.
Teachers track progress through learning outcome–aligned exit tickets, project evaluations, peer feedback, self-reflection, class discussions, and diagnostics. Assessments for COMP3 are curriculum-based rather than waiting for standardized tests.
AI literacy is woven throughout instruction.
Students learn how AI works, its applications and limitations, ethical considerations, and how to use AI tools responsibly in computational thinking and projects — not as a separate add-on, but embedded throughout the learning sequence.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Available curricula rarely cover all learning outcomes. Most require stitching multiple resources together, which risks gaps and incoherence.
Design response
Map available curricula against the six outcome domains explicitly. Identify gaps and plan targeted supplementation or custom units for uncovered areas.
Challenge
Traditional CS instruction tends to be isolating rather than collaborative, with individual students working independently on screens.
Design response
Build in pair programming, group projects, and structured discussions as core instructional strategies — not enrichment. Collaboration is a learning outcome, not a reward.
Challenge
Schools can overemphasize projects at the expense of foundational skills, leaving students without the conceptual grounding that makes projects meaningful.
Design response
Balance extended projects with explicit instruction, structured practice, and varied activities. Projects should culminate a learning sequence, not replace it.

School & Classroom Culture, Routines & Norms
The shared beliefs, expectations, and patterns of interaction that shape how students experience learning every day.
Leaps most connected to this element
Whole-child focus Connection & community Agency
Outcomes this element most directly develops
5 · Digital Literacy 6 · Career Learning 1 · AI Literacy
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Every student belongs in computing.
Inclusive language, diverse role models, and celebrating varied approaches to problems signal to every student — especially those historically excluded from CS — that they are capable computer scientists and AI users.
COMP3 connects to real-world topics students care about.
Computational thinking is grounded in issues students find meaningful — climate change, social justice, community needs, and AI ethics. CS topics that connect the real world to the school community are visualized throughout the school.
Routines build collaboration and digital wellness.
Classroom norms emphasize peer support and productive struggle. Schools promote healthy technology habits, balanced screen time, and mindful use of digital tools — including AI.
AI and emerging technologies are ongoing conversations.
Students, families, and staff engage regularly in discussions about AI's impact, ethical computing, and responsible technology use — not as one-time lessons, but woven into school culture.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
CS culture often defaults to a narrow "programmer" identity that excludes students who don't self-identify with tech — particularly girls, students of color, and students from under-resourced communities.
Design response
Actively surface diverse computing role models, celebrate multiple ways of solving problems, and connect CS to domains students already care about. Representation in materials and classroom visuals matters.
Challenge
Productive failure — essential in CS — can undermine student confidence if classrooms aren't built to normalize struggle and iteration.
Design response
Build explicit norms around debugging, revision, and learning from errors. Frame "my code doesn't work" as a normal, expected part of the process — not a sign of incapacity.

Schedules & Use of Time
How instructional time is allocated, structured, and protected for CS and AI learning.
Leaps most connected to this element
High expectations & rigor Relevance Customization
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Adequate time is allocated for COMP3 instruction.
Students engage in 2–5 hours per week of COMP3 instruction. The master schedule protects this time with the same priority as core subjects.
COMP3 is integrated across subjects and schedules.
Computational thinking and AI literacy are woven into math, science, ELA, and social studies — not siloed to a single CS period. Cross-curricular integration reinforces learning and increases total exposure.
Flexible instructional structures allow for differentiation.
Station rotations, weekly digital citizenship lessons, quarterly COMP3 projects, and CS explorations give teachers tools to reach different learners. Schedules accommodate core instruction, enrichment, and intervention.
Professional learning time is built into the schedule.
Staff schedules include dedicated time for COMP3 training, collaborative planning, and ongoing learning about emerging technologies including AI.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
CS lacks core subject status in most states, making it easy to cut when schedules are tight or competing priorities emerge.
Design response
Anchor CS time to existing schedule structures. Make it non-negotiable at the master schedule level, visible in the school's public communication about what it offers.
Challenge
2–5 hours per week is a wide range. Without clear intent, schools may underallocate time and call it sufficient.
Design response
Map time explicitly against the learning outcomes you're committing to. Fewer outcomes with more time is better than all outcomes with insufficient time.

Adult Roles, Hiring & Learning
What adults do, what they need to know and believe, how they are hired, and how they are supported to grow.
Leaps most connected to this element
High expectations & rigor Connection & community Whole-child focus
Goals for this element
  • Educators believe deeply in every student's potential to excel in computing and feel shared responsibility to broaden participation — regardless of background, prior achievement, or whether students see themselves as "tech people."
  • All educators responsible for COMP3 instruction possess strong content knowledge and pedagogical expertise, including the ability to anticipate students' thinking and address misconceptions.
  • Collaboration among educators is routine and purposeful — grounded in shared materials and student work, enabling consistent practice across classrooms.
  • Professional learning is ongoing, curriculum-embedded, and directly connected to upcoming instruction — sustained long enough in duration and intensity to meaningfully build CS content knowledge and self-efficacy.
  • Educators and leaders understand that COMP3 education is a rapidly evolving field, and professional learning systems are designed to keep pace with changes in content, pedagogy, tools, and AI.
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
COMP3 leadership roles are clearly established.
A specialized COMP3 teacher with CS expertise, an IT specialist for tech management, and a STEM+COMP3 administrator oversee program development. Principals, APs, and instructional coaches actively support COMP3 implementation.
Professional development is continuous, curriculum-aligned, and AI-inclusive.
Teachers receive training that builds capacity to understand AI tools, teach AI concepts appropriately, and integrate AI responsibly into COMP3 instruction. PD includes coaching cycles, lesson internalization protocols, and PLCs.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Only 48% of teachers currently feel equipped to teach AI, and 88% say they need more professional learning resources. AI advances faster than most PD systems can keep up.
Design response
Build a sustained, multi-year PD system rather than one-time workshops. Partner with curriculum providers for embedded support. Connect teachers to professional networks like CSTA.
Challenge
CS teachers are often isolated — the only person in their building teaching the subject — which limits collaboration, coaching, and shared improvement.
Design response
Create cross-school or cross-district PLCs for CS teachers. Use virtual collaboration tools to connect isolated educators. Designate a CS instructional lead even in small settings.

Technology & Tech Infrastructure
Devices, connectivity, software, and the systems that keep them working equitably and reliably.
Leaps most connected to this element
High expectations & rigor Customization Relevance
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Equitable access to devices and connectivity is guaranteed.
A 1:1 device-to-student ratio with reliable WiFi at school and home. Device refresh schedules prevent equity gaps from opening up over time as hardware ages.
Technology procurement is aligned to curriculum.
All hardware and software purchases directly support curriculum requirements and are sustainable beyond individual teachers. AI platforms and software are evaluated for long-term viability — not just grant-period coverage.
Systems manage technology reliably.
An LMS organizes coursework, clear procedures govern device distribution and security, and digital citizenship policies are in place. Tech support with clear troubleshooting protocols serves students, families, and staff.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
AI educational tools proliferate faster than schools can evaluate them. Without quality control frameworks, schools risk adopting tools that are pedagogically weak, inequitable, or unsustainable.
Design response
Use the Curriculum Evaluator Tool and AI Curriculum Airtable to assess tools against criteria including alignment to COMP3 outcomes, equity features, and evidence of effectiveness.
Challenge
Home connectivity gaps persist, particularly for students in under-resourced communities — creating inequitable access to AI tools and computing practice outside school hours.
Design response
Audit connectivity before assigning home-based computing work. Pursue hotspot programs, partner with community wifi providers, and design instruction that does not penalize students for limited home access.

Space & Facilities
How physical learning environments are designed, arranged, and equipped to support the full range of COMP3 learning modes.
Leaps most connected to this element
Connection & community High expectations & rigor Customization
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Spaces support the full range of COMP3 learning modes.
Effective computing spaces accommodate pair programming, group projects, design sprints, debugging sessions, hardware building, and whole-class discussion. A traditional 2005 computer lab rows configuration serves none of these well.
Physical computing materials are treated as core infrastructure.
Robotics kits, microcontrollers, sensors, and maker tools are budgeted on the same cycle as textbooks, with assigned maintenance responsibility and documented checkout systems. Distributing materials to classrooms reduces scheduling friction and signals that physical computing is regular instruction.
Flexible furniture enables multiple configurations.
Modular furniture reconfigurable in under two minutes, power access across the room, large shared displays visible from all positions, adequate material storage, and writable surfaces for collaborative design.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Computing educators often inherit spaces not designed for their pedagogy. Making the case for reconfiguration requires explaining why an existing "computer lab" is insufficient.
Design response
Audit existing spaces against the full range of COMP3 learning modes. Use the audit to generate specific renovation requests, not general concerns. Spatial disparities are a learning equity issue.

Family & Community Partnerships
How schools engage caregivers and communities as partners in students' computing and AI learning.
Leaps most connected to this element
Connection & community Relevance Agency
Goals for this element
  • Families are informed early, often, and specifically about what AI is being used in class, why, and what student data it touches — recognizing that as of 2025, 96% of families with elementary-aged children reported receiving no AI-related communication from their school.
  • Communication about CS and AI instruction is bidirectional and ongoing — allowing families to ask questions and share concerns as tools evolve.
  • Families are meaningfully engaged as partners in their children's computing and AI learning — with opportunities to understand what students are doing, why it matters, and how to support learning at home.
  • Engagement practices are designed to reach all families, including those facing language barriers, schedule constraints, limited digital access, or historical marginalization from school systems.
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Families are partners, not recipients of information.
COMP3 nights, workshops on supporting computational thinking at home, and opportunities to participate in showcases position caregivers as active participants. Their expertise about their children and communities informs the design.
Real-world learning connects students to community and careers.
Guest speakers, workplace tours, student mentorships, and community-based projects connect students to COMP3 applications and career pathways — making learning relevant and broadening participation.
AI communication is proactive and transparent.
Schools provide regular updates when new tools are introduced, explain what data is collected and how it is protected, and offer ready-made communication templates so information quality doesn't depend on individual teacher initiative.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Many caregivers have limited technical backgrounds and may experience AI anxiety — which can undermine classroom practices if schools don't address it directly.
Design response
Offer adult-only sessions where caregivers experience CS and AI activities themselves. Acknowledge uncertainty openly — families who feel included in the learning process trust schools more than those who feel information is being managed.

Budget & Operations
How resources are allocated, sustained, and diversified to support a durable COMP3+AI program.
Leaps most connected to this element
High expectations & rigor Relevance
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
COMP3 essentials are funded sustainably.
Budgets cover technology, staff, professional development, curriculum, and infrastructure — annually, not just in year one. Initial and ongoing costs are planned explicitly rather than assumed to decrease.
AI tool funding extends beyond grant periods.
New AI platforms and software are evaluated for sustainability. Schools avoid building dependence on short-term funding sources that disappear in 2–3 years, displacing both the tools and the teacher capacity built around them.
Staffing resources are allocated strategically.
Dedicated COMP3 teachers, specialists across grade levels, planning time for integration, instructional coaches, and coordinators for secondary pathways are all funded as core positions — not add-ons.
Funding sources are diverse.
Local partnerships, state and federal grants (including new NSF and USDOE investments in AI education), and community fundraising supplement core allocations — reducing vulnerability to any single funding source.

Continuous Learning & Improvement
The systems and routines used to define success, measure progress, and reflect together to improve quality and impact.
Leaps most connected to this element
High expectations & rigor Connection & community
Goals for this element
  • Improvement efforts are sustained, focused, and cumulative — schools select a small number of high-leverage instructional priorities and pursue them over multiple years rather than cycling through disconnected initiatives.
  • Assessment practices serve instructional purposes — providing timely, actionable feedback to both students and teachers about conceptual understanding, computational thinking, and coding proficiency.
  • Equity is an explicit and ongoing object of continuous improvement — using participation and outcomes data to identify and address structural barriers.
  • CS and AI improvement efforts are connected to broader professional networks and research-practice partnerships that can supply curriculum-aligned assessments and cross-school learning no single school can generate alone.
What it looks like in a strong COMP3+AI design
Data systems are aligned to COMP3 learning outcomes.
LMS and data review processes track COMP3 learning outcomes, culture, perception, and student engagement — not just usage metrics.
Evaluation and adaptation happen regularly.
COMP3 classroom observations, data review meetings, and surveys monitor implementation fidelity, workload, and educator mindsets. Findings drive adjustments, not just documentation.
AI tool effectiveness is continuously evaluated.
How AI platforms and tools are used in instruction, their impact on student learning and teacher practice, and stakeholder feedback on AI integration all inform ongoing decisions — not just adoption.
Common challenges & design responses
Challenge
Assessment instruments for CS and AI literacy are still in development. Districts often evaluate AI education through usage metrics rather than learning outcomes.
Design response
Develop curriculum-based assessments rather than waiting for standardized tests. Use student work, debugging logs, and project portfolios as evidence of learning. Connect to research-practice partnerships developing better instruments.
Transcend partners with communities to create and spread extraordinary learning for all.
